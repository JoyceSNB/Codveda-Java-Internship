The Internet of Things, often shortened to IoT, describes a network
of physical devices that exchange data over the internet. Sensors
collect information about the world, and actuators change something
about that world in response.

A water quality monitoring system is a clear example. Sensors read
temperature, pH, ammonia, and turbidity. The data travels to a broker,
gets stored in a time series database, and appears on a dashboard.
When the data crosses a threshold, an actuator responds: a heater
switches on, or a valve opens.

The interesting part is not the sensors. The interesting part is the
decision logic sitting between the sensors and the actuators. A simple
threshold is brittle. Real water does not behave like a step function.
It drifts, it fluctuates, and a reading that is fine at noon may be a
problem at midnight.

This is why fuzzy logic is useful. Instead of asking whether the water
is good or bad, fuzzy logic asks how good and how bad, and how much
each answer should matter. The result is a system that responds the way
a careful person would respond, rather than the way a thermostat does.

Data alone is not knowledge. Knowledge is what happens when data meets
a decision, and a decision meets the world.
